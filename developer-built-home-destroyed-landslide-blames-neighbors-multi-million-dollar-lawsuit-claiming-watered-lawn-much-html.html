Developer who built home destroyed in landslide blames the neighbors in multi-million dollar lawsuit

Posted by Reinaldo Massengill on Monday, March 25, 2024

The developer who built a home destroyed in a landslide last year has blamed the neighbors in a multi-million dollar lawsuit - claiming they watered the lawn so much it caused the soil to give way.

Around 20 homes were evacuated during a heavy rainstorm on August 5, 2014, after falling rock and debris crumbled a residence in North Salt Lake, Utah.

A year on, no one has claimed responsibility for the land slip, but now Eaglepointe Development and SKY Properties have denied any wrongdoing, instead accusing residents, businesses and infrastructure firms who worked in the area.

This home was destroyed, prompting a standoff between the developers, residents and city officials which has now lasted a year. They believe it will cost $2million to stabilize the hillside, but no one was claimed responsibility for what happened 

According to a counter-suit, seen by the Desert News, they have blamed; a natural gas company that owns and operates two pipelines near the slide, two geotech firms that studied the land prior to further development and the local tennis club for expanding a parking lot at the base of the hill.

But they have also said the actions of Paul and Jeanette Evans directly contributed to the landslide.

Their home is now dangling on the edge of the unstable hillside after they lost 60ft of their back yard in the slip.

In response to an original lawsuit filed by Kern River that points to the developer's actions for the damage, Eaglepointe accuses the Evans family of using 'nearly two-and-a-half times more gallons per year than their neighbor who owns a comparably sized lot,'

The documents say their 'Historical practice of introducing excessive amounts of water into the soils and hillside … resulted in water migrating into the hillside … contributing to the instability of the soil.' 

Mr Evans said he was 'shocked' at the claims by the multi-million dollar companies. He believes he has been scapegoated for his outcry about the scandal after a year of frustration. 

While insisting the company has no evidence to suggest he partly caused the landslide, he says has also been told the comparison with those living next-door isn't even accurate.  

The developers claim Paul and Jeanette Evans contributed to the incident because they allegedly put excessive amounts of water on their back yard. The pair, whose home (right) is hanging perilously next to the edge of the unstable hill, say the company is 'grasping at straws'

While insisting the company has no evidence to suggest he partly caused the landslide (pictured), Mr Evans says has also been told the comparison with those living next-door isn't even accurate

He told the paper: 'They're simply grasping at straws and trying to divert attention from their own actions.

'Hasn't the Evans family been through enough already. It needs to be clear that the hillside was moving well before the landslide.'

The couple have constantly called developers what they are going to do about the development, but they are yet to receive a response. 

It will cost roughly $2million to fix the hillside - by either building a retaining wall or softening the slope by filling in crevices. 

North Salt Lake City Manager Barry Edwards says officials are 'anxious' to get the matter resolved and the finger-pointing doesn't help. 

Mr Evans says developers were warned of cracks in 2012 and 2013, but they carried on building regardless, and insists building on the hillside above his home ultimate caused the landslide. 

According to the lawsuit, Eaglepointe wants the 'responsible parties' to pay for the damages.  

The Eagleridge Swim and Tennis Club (pictured) has also been blamed for expanding their parking lot at the base of the hill
