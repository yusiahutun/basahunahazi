A Connecticut man has been arrested for allegedly compiling a trove of naked pictures of girls as young as 12 in his small town and then trading them online.

Police say Nicholas O. Olsen, 22, collected the naked pictures of 20 Plainville, Connecticut, women, usually getting them from their ex-boyfriends. He also took some of the photos himself, or received them from women he was dating.

Police identified 20 Plainville women whose photos were posted on an online forum Olsen allegedly frequented, WFSB-TV reports.

Arrested: Nicholas Olsen, 22, is charged with possession of child pornography, eight counts of promoting a minor in an obscene performance and second-degree sexual assault and risk of injury to a minor

Sixteen of the women in the photos were between age 12 and 16 when the pictures were taken - making them child pornography.

Plainville is a city of less than 18,000 outside Hartford, Connecticut.

Many of the photos were 'selfies' that the women had taken and sent along to boyfriends - but they never intended them to end up online or even in Olsen's hands.

Investigators also allege that Olsen had sex with one of the women in the pictures, who was 14 or 15 at the time. Olsen was 18 or 19 at the time.

Investigators say Olsen used an online forum, which is hosted outside the U.S., to exchange the photos anonymously with people across the Internet.

'Mr. Olsen thrived on the power and control he had over the fact that he possessed these young females' photographs,' Plainville police Detective Sergeant Nicholas Mullins told WFSB-TV.

Investigators say Olsen posted the pictures online starting in 2011. However, man of the images are five to 10 years old.

One of the women in the images contacted police after she saw her photo online.

Police said they could only prosecute Olsen for the images that allegedly showed underage women.

They said it underscores the dangers of women taking naked 'selfies' and sending them to boyfriends or anyone else.

'These young females have to realize that when they take a nude photo of themselves, and they share it with somebody, that photograph is time-stamped forever,' Detective Mullins told the Hartford Courant.

'That image is not going away. It's not going to disappear, and it's going to haunt some of the girls years and years later.'
